Key Singapore Financial Reporting Standards (SFRS) for Private Limited Companies

SOURCE: SFRS issued by the Accounting Standards Council (ASC), Singapore

---

SFRS 1-1 — PRESENTATION OF FINANCIAL STATEMENTS

Requires a complete set of financial statements with comparative information.
Key principles: fair presentation, going concern, accrual basis, consistency of presentation, materiality, aggregation.
Prohibits offsetting of assets/liabilities or income/expenses unless permitted by another SFRS.

---

SFRS 1-2 — INVENTORIES

Inventories measured at the lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV).
Cost includes purchase price, conversion costs, and other costs to bring inventories to present location and condition.
Cost formulas: FIFO (first-in, first-out) or weighted average cost. LIFO is not permitted.
NRV is the estimated selling price less estimated costs of completion and estimated costs to make the sale.
Write-down to NRV is recognised as an expense in the period of write-down.

---

SFRS 1-7 — STATEMENT OF CASH FLOWS

Requires disclosure of changes in cash and cash equivalents during the period.
Operating activities: cash from the principal revenue-producing activities.
  - Direct method: shows major classes of gross cash receipts and payments
  - Indirect method: adjusts profit/loss for effects of non-cash transactions, deferrals/accruals, investing/financing items
Investing activities: acquisition/disposal of long-term assets and other investments.
Financing activities: activities that change the equity capital and borrowing structure.

---

SFRS 1-8 — ACCOUNTING POLICIES, CHANGES IN ACCOUNTING ESTIMATES AND ERRORS

Accounting policies: specific principles, bases, conventions, rules applied in preparing financial statements.
Changes in accounting policy: apply retrospectively unless impracticable.
Changes in accounting estimates: apply prospectively (current and future periods).
Material prior period errors: correct retrospectively by restating comparative amounts.

---

SFRS 1-10 — EVENTS AFTER THE REPORTING PERIOD

Adjusting events: provide evidence of conditions that existed at the reporting date — adjust financial statements.
Non-adjusting events: arise after the reporting date — disclose if material.
Examples of non-adjusting events: declaration of dividends after the reporting date (disclose but do not accrue), major business combinations, natural disasters.
Dividends declared after the reporting date must not be recognised as a liability at reporting date.

---

SFRS 1-12 — INCOME TAXES

Requires recognition of current and deferred tax.
Current tax: amount payable/recoverable for the current period based on taxable profit.
Deferred tax: temporary differences between carrying amount of assets/liabilities and their tax base.
Deferred tax liability: recognised for all taxable temporary differences (with limited exceptions).
Deferred tax asset: recognised to the extent it is probable that sufficient future taxable profit will be available.
Tax rate: use the rate enacted or substantively enacted at the reporting date (Singapore CIT rate: 17%).

SFRS 1-16 — PROPERTY, PLANT AND EQUIPMENT (PPE)

Recognition: item of PPE recognised when it is probable that future economic benefits will flow to the entity and the cost can be measured reliably.
Initial measurement: at cost (purchase price + directly attributable costs to bring asset to intended use).
Subsequent measurement: cost model (cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment) or revaluation model.
Cost model is most commonly used by Singapore SMEs.

DEPRECIATION
Depreciation allocated systematically over the useful life of the asset.
Depreciable amount: cost less residual value.
Methods:
  - Straight-line method: equal annual charge over useful life (most common)
  - Diminishing (reducing) balance method: constant percentage applied to carrying amount
  - Units of production method: based on expected use or output
The depreciation method must reflect the expected pattern of consumption of future economic benefits.
The method used must be disclosed in accounting policies.
Depreciation begins when the asset is available for use and ceases when derecognised.
Useful life and residual value reviewed at each financial year end.
Change in depreciation method treated as change in accounting estimate (prospective).

Common useful lives for Singapore SMEs:
  - Buildings: 25–50 years
  - Plant and machinery: 5–15 years
  - Office equipment/computers: 3–5 years
  - Motor vehicles: 5 years
  - Furniture and fittings: 5–10 years

DERECOGNITION
Carrying amount derecognised on disposal or when no future economic benefits expected.
Gain or loss on disposal: difference between net disposal proceeds and carrying amount.
Gain or loss recognised in profit or loss.

SFRS 1-36 — IMPAIRMENT OF ASSETS

At each reporting date, assess whether there is any indication that an asset may be impaired.
If indication exists, estimate the recoverable amount.
Recoverable amount: higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use.
Impairment loss: when carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount. Recognised in profit or loss.
Reversal of impairment: permitted when conditions have changed, except for goodwill.

---

SFRS 1-37 — PROVISIONS, CONTINGENT LIABILITIES AND CONTINGENT ASSETS

Provision: present obligation as a result of a past event; probable outflow of resources; reliable estimate possible.
Contingent liability: possible obligation (not probable) or present obligation where payment not probable or amount cannot be reliably estimated. Disclosed in notes; not recognised.
Contingent asset: possible asset from past events; not recognised; disclosed when inflow is probable.

---

SFRS 1-38 — INTANGIBLE ASSETS

Recognised when identifiable, controlled by entity, and expected to generate future economic benefits.
Examples: computer software, patents, trademarks, customer lists, franchises.
Initial measurement at cost.
Subsequent: cost model or revaluation model (revaluation only if active market exists).
Useful life: finite (amortise over useful life) or indefinite (no amortisation; test annually for impairment).
Most intangible assets in Singapore SMEs have finite useful lives.

SFRS 15 — REVENUE FROM CONTRACTS WITH CUSTOMERS

Five-step model for revenue recognition:
1. Identify the contract with a customer
2. Identify the performance obligations
3. Determine the transaction price
4. Allocate the transaction price to performance obligations
5. Recognise revenue when (or as) performance obligations are satisfied

Revenue recognised at a point in time (e.g. goods delivered) or over time (e.g. services performed).
Variable consideration (discounts, rebates, refunds) included in transaction price to the extent it is highly probable no significant revenue reversal will occur.

---

SFRS 16 — LEASES

Lessees must recognise right-of-use (ROU) asset and lease liability for most leases.
Practical expedients available: short-term leases (12 months or less) and low-value assets may be expensed.
Finance cost recognised on lease liability using effective interest method.
ROU asset depreciated over shorter of asset useful life or lease term.
Impacts balance sheet (higher assets and liabilities) and P&L (depreciation + interest instead of rent expense).
